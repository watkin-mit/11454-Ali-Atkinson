Migration's Mutual Benefits



Ilham Ali and Will Atkinson

Growing steadily, migration from Central American countries in the Northern Triangle -- made up of Guatemala, Honduras, and El Salvador -- has been covered widely in the popular press. Commentators postulate widely on the effects of migration for a local economy, or the geopolitical relations between home and destination countries, or on the meaning of sweeping statements made about immigrant groups. But by and large, the dynamics of migration for the individual are not well understood.

Join us as we explore these journeys and their mutual benefits -- from local economies to the migrants' families back home.

The Journey

Immigrants from Guatemala, Honduras, and El Salvador go on to live in varied places across the United States.

The top counties in the US are Harris County, Texas (in the Houston metroplex) and Los Angeles County, California.

Many also settle in areas surrounding the capital in Washington, D.C. and in the Miami area in southern Florida.

Each of these areas have varying cultures and lifestyles. Cost of living can differ significantly as well. The map details the top three US counties that become home for migrants arriving from each of the Northern Triangle nations.

Local Jobs

According to recent survey data from the United Nations World Food Programme (UN WFP), more than half of migrants continue their past type of work in the destination country. These workers bring valuable experience to their new communities, while others change roles in their new home. Data shows that unemployment is very low among these populations -- under 1% in most cases -- and a small fraction of the U.S. average as a whole. These, and other transitions in work lifestyle, represent big changes for the migrants who experience them.











For instance, some agricultural workers go to work in informal labor sectors, such as chicken plants and factories. Other former agricultural workers go on to be salaried employees in various sectors like transportation.













Others continue in informal work, move to salaried positions, or start their own businesses.





















And a large number of students find employment upon arrival, making their early entrance into the workforce and starting careers.

Family Needs

Having found work in their new community, migrants frequently send back some income to their families back home in the form of remittances. According to the UN, these transfers represent just 15 percent of what migrants earn on average -- meaning that the vast majority of their earnings can benefit local economies in the US. Nonetheless, remittances are a critical income source for many relatives back home -- supporting one in nine families globally and about one in four in the UN WFP survey of Northern Triangle households.

By understanding the journey to a new location, career, and lifestyle, we can start to learn more about the daily experiences of migrants who are adjusting to a wave of changes. We hope to begin telling a more nuanced story about the realities and decisions these people face.

What could this information lead to? By highlighting the immense challenge of migration and employment, these findings could support calls for social protection programs (to stabilize home country conditions) as well as improved legal pathways for those who do wish to migrate. And despite the increasing role of remittances, transaction costs are reducing the direct flow of funds to families in need.
