Mapping Migration's Mutual Benefits



Ilham Ali and Will Atkinson

Growing steadily, migration from Central American countries in the Northern Triangle -- made up of Guatemala, Honduras, and El Salvador -- has been covered widely in the popular press. Commentators postulate widely on the effects of migration for a local economy, or the geopolitical relations between home and destination countries, or on the meaning of sweeping statements made about immigrant groups. However, by and large, the dynamics of migration for the individual are not well understood.

Join us as we explore these journeys and their mutual benefits -- from local economies to the migrants' families. Our outline will be as follows:









THE JOURNEY: where migrants settle in the United States

LOCAL ECONOMIES: where migrants join the local workforce

GLOBAL FAMILIES: how migrants support their faraway homes

Immigrants from Guatemala, Honduras, and El Salvador go on to live in varied places across the United States.



The top counties in the US are Harris County, Texas (in the Houston metroplex) and Los Angeles County, California.



Many also settle in areas surrounding the capital in Washington, D.C. and in the Miami area in southern Florida.



Each of these areas have varying cultures and lifestyles. Cost of living can differ significantly as well. The map details the top three US counties that become home for migrants arriving from each of the Northern Triangle nations.











While some migrants continue their past type of work in the United States, others find new roles after completing their journey.



For instance, some agricultural workers go to work in informal labor sectors, such as chicken plants and factories. Other former agricultural workers go on to be salaried employees in various sectors like transportation.



A large number of students find employment upon arrival, making their early entrance into the workforce and starting careers. Data shows that unemployment is very low among these populations -- under 1% in most cases -- and a small fraction of the US average as a whole. These, and other transitions in work lifestyle, represent big changes for the migrants who experience them.



By understanding the journey to a new career, lifestyle, and region, we can start to learn more about the daily experiences of migrants who are adjusting to a wave of changes. We hope to begin telling a more nuanced story about the realities and decisions faced through insight on the journey, work-life, and (eventually) financial balances experienced.
